Early Detection of Lower MMSE Scores in Elderly Based on Dual-Task Gait

[bib] [doi]

Objective: The dual-task paradigm is a promising procedure for estimating cognitive status, and may also be collaterally used to reduce cognitive decline and prevent dementia. In this research, we use the Mini-Mental State Exam (MMSE) to assess cognitive status in the elderly as a reference, and investigate a potential of using machine learning for early detecting cognitive impairment in elderly. Methods: Although many studies have suggested that dual-task performance, in which participants perform a cognitive task while walking, is associated with cognition, they only considered the correlation between cognitive parameters and simple gait feature, such as gait speed, through statistical analysis. We instead use a Kinect sensor to capture participants’ whole-body movements and extract a rich gait feature that has the ability to exhibit different tendencies of movements between healthy and cognitive-impaired elderlies. Results: In our experiments, a classifier based on the dual-task gait feature achieved higher performance than the one based on single-task feature; the performance of the rich gait feature was better than that of a simple one; and an optimal detection performance was achieved with a MMSE cut-off score of 25. Conclusion: We positively validated that the proposed method could early detect elderly with lower MMSE scores based on dual-task gait feature with a promising performance. Significance: Our approach can support an early and automated diagnosis of cognitive impairment.
